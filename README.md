# Computer-Science--CS250

Project Snapshot
The project, SNHU Travel, sought to revamp how people go about planning their travel. The problem we set out to solve with Scrum Agile development was how clunky traditional travel booking systems have become. Through Scrum Agile, I helped develop a Minimum Viable Product that eliminated the unnecessary and focused only on what today’s traveler needs.

Tuning in to User Needs
As a way of avoiding the trap of building something that might not resonate with users, we sought user needs as a guiding principle. As a liaison between tech development and stakeholders, we did the following:
- Engaged with focus groups to get a sense of what users want in terms of “top destinations” and “profile settings.”
- Avoided risks by engaging with user feedback.
- Used User Stories as a way of converting user needs into a working product.

The Power of User Stories
User Stories changed the way we go about building a product. Instead of building something because we can, we focus on the why before we get into the how. This way, we ensure that we get a working product that meets user needs, like ensuring that a user receives an email confirmation within 60 seconds of payment.

My Development Approach and Future Agile Goals
As a developer, I believe that flexibility and constant movement forward are essential. As a result, we did not have a traditional “plan” for the project, and we constantly refined our product backlog to ensure that we remained relevant and in touch with the ever-changing needs of the travel industry.

Future Goals with Agile Development
As a way of building on what we have learned with Scrum Agile, I hope to further utilize:
- Information Radiators like Kanban boards, which provide a single source of truth that keeps everyone aligned without having to hold meetings.
- Sprint Goals, which serve as a guiding principle that reminds us that we must ensure that our daily activities contribute to the bigger picture.
- Feedback Loops, which help us remain honest with ourselves and ensure that we get a working product.

Being a Solid Team Player
Being a good team player is more than just writing clean code. From my experience working with the SNHU Travel team, it means: 

Encouraging transparency: It is about being comfortable with admitting to the team that an API, or a bug, is tripping you up, and working through it as a team.

Proactive dialogue: Much like with our testers, it is not just about identifying issues, but actively seeking clarification on validation logic to ensure everything is running smoothly.

Collective responsibility: It is about taking ownership of the project’s success as a team, and everyone feeling invested in the outcome.
